Even though I would usually identify myself as a STEM student, I do enjoy literature a lot! I LOVE being immersed in fictional worlds created by talented writers, living the lives of different characters, while expressing myself through writing as well✍️

In June 2023, I wrote something of a combination of philosphy and psychology essay (which are the two topics I've never tried my entire life🤯) for the John Locke Essay Competition. But the essay turned out okay since I read tons of papers before hand.

This topic I chose was :"Are beliefs voluntary". Here's a peek at my essay's opening👀

[Would you believe that I was a giant grasshopper if I offered you 1000 pounds? This ludicrous yet thought-provoking question was brought up by Anthony Booth to reason against doxastic voluntarism, a philosophical view that people have control over their own beliefs.i While doxastic voluntarism has been a traditional view defended by Descartes, Augustine, Aquinas, Pascal, and others throughout history, those with a more modern view of the world (who tend to enjoy higher economic, educational, and social status) tend to support the opposite. But why is there a divergence in epistemic stances in the psychological and philosophic literature, and why did doxastic involuntarism win prevalence as time advanced?]

I believe the most eccentric literary analysis I've ever written might have been what I wrote for the poem Juggler 🤡

For me the poem didn't make much sense at first glance...but as I read it for a few more times, I was slowly able to feel what Richard Wilbur was trying to convey, or at least come up with my own interpretation:

[Most humans tend to gravitate towards negativity. Oftentimes our storage of positive energies can be easily overwhelmed by the little, negative ones, because they tend to be spontaneously magnified. It almost seems like many of the things we do are destined to fail, and this calls for the need of someone or even something to rejuvenate us, combat the invisible pull of negativity, and put our lived back on track. In the poem Juggler, Richard Wilbur created a speaker who depicted a lively and delightful image of a juggler tossing objects in front of the audience. By intertwining various metaphors and special syntax and perfect rhymes with the imagery throughout the poem, the speaker alludes to our natural gravitation to negativity, and proves the unnoticed effect of such rejuvenation on our lives.]

Here comes my favourite essay--the old Pride and Prejudice🩵

I wouldn't deny that Pride and Prejudice is probably one of most clichéd book you can write about for your literature class assignment, but there's a reason why it's SO TIMELESS.

Behind all of its classic characters and wonderful plot twists is the talented, daring, progressive, feminist author Jane Austen//

After watching the film Becoming Jane Austen, I was somehow able to perceive the author's work from an entirely new angle🔭 and thus I immediately wrote an essay comparing Austen's life with the plot development of Pride and Prejudice. Now looking back, I'm not completely satisfied with this essay since it was written a few years ago when I had a quite shallow view of the world, yet I still find the essay a great milestone in my writing career(?)
