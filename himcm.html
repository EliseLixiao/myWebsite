models summary:

  • First, we used a simple equation to calculate the average 10-year increase of CO2 concentration for each year. Our result shows that instead of 2004, the 2003 increase of CO2 resulted in a larger increase than observed over any previous 10-year period.
  • Second, we started with building three models that analyze the trend of CO2 concentration level: Linear Regression, Quadratic Regression, and ARIMA (Auto-regressive Integrated Moving Average Model). We were given data points of 63 years. To evaluate the accuracy of our models‚Äô prediction, we selected the first 53 data points for modeling, and left the last 10 for F-test. None of the models agrees with the prediction that the CO2 concentration level will reach 685 ppm by 2050. The threshold of 685 ppm wouldn‚Äôt be met until 2213, 2100, and 2101 according to the prediction of the three models respectively.
  • Third, we adopted the superior model ARIMA when studying the trend of changes in temper- ature. The model predicts that the average land-ocean temperature will change by 1.25¬∞C, 1.50¬∞C, and 2¬∞C in 2031, 2048, and 2081 respectively.
  • Fourth, through collecting data online, we learned that there is a linear relationship between Radiative Forcing and temperature changes. We first established the relationship between CO2 concentration and Radiative Forcing while considering additional factors such as Solar Irradiance and greenhouse gases. Then we linearly regressed the estimated Radiative Forcing and the given temperature changes, and compared both its fitness and its prediction accuracy with a model that directly linearly regress CO2 concentration and temperature changes. It turned out that both the fitness and the prediction accuracy of the complex model was lower than the direct linear model.
  • Lastly, we concluded the possible flaws in our model, and pointed out the factors that might influence its application into the future.
