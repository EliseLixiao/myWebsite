MY JHU BMEI EXPERIENCE

JHU BMEI (Johns Hopkins Biomedical Engineering Innovation) is a pre-college program designed for students who are interested in biomedical engineering!

check out their website to learn more about the program ->

My experience with the JHU BMEI program in the summer of 2022 might have been the most demanding academic experience I’ve ever had😣 The program had a quite intensive schedule which required us to finish a report or a project almost every week. At the end of the program, I completed 5️⃣ projects!

Though the experience was a bit stressful and tiring, the various useful skills I acquired by the end of it led to an ecstatic sense of achievement. The program helped my truly envision my future career as a biomedical engineer:)

The following are the details of my five projects:

Human Efficiency Model

motivation: For closed units such as spaceship🚀or submarine🤿, accurately calculating the necessary amount of oxygen is crucial to the survival and work efficiency of the people on board. However, because of the difference in each individual’s body weight and workload, it’s hard to estimate total oxygen use. By exploring the relationship between these factors and work efficiency, oxygen consumption in special scenarios can be better predicted.

method: I did three groups of experiment. In each group, I walked 20, 40, and 60 steps of stairs and measured my heart rate (bpm), ventilation (L/breath), and time (min). I took 0kg load, 5kg load, and 10kg load with me in the three groups of experiment respectively. I converted ventilation and the number of steps into input and output energy, and used those recorded data to estimate human efficiency in different scenarios. Eventually I graphed the relationship between human efficiency and its influencing factors.

results: I found a near linear relationship between energy output and input regardless of total weight, a negative linear relationship between work output and human efficiency❗and that human efficiency can either decrease or increase with the increase of weight in different ranges.

Cardiovascular System Model

motivation: Oftentimes, the human body is so complex that it’s hard to study the rate of blood flow and the variables on which it depends🤔 Using electric circuit as model can largely simplify the problem, helping researchers to study the rate of blood flow, the results of which may be applied to other fields of biology or medicine.

method: There are two models in our experiment. In the first model we arranged four resistors ⏛ in parallel circuit and study the relationship between rate of blood flow and blood vessel length by changing the number of resistors in series, and study the relationship between rate of blood flow and area of blood vessel’s cross section by changing the number of resistors in parallel. In the second model, we arranged six resistors ⏛ in a parallel circuit with six paths---each resistor representing a type of blood vessel in the pulmonary circuit---and recorded the current in each path to graph the change in rate of blood flow in the pulmonary vascular system.

results: As expected, I found that the rate of blood flow, which is the independent variable, has a positive linear relationship with area of blood vessel’s cross section, and the length of blood vessel is inversely proportional to the rate of flow.

Arduino Project

abstract: The goal of this project was to build an alert device in which an LED light💡 is turned on whenever a beam of laser light is covered. It has a laser emitter and a dual-color LED as outputs, and a button and a photoresistor as inputs. The photoresistor is constantly covered by a box📦 When the button is pressed, the laser emits a beam of light into the box. The threshold is met and the LED is thus turned off. However, when there’s an obstacle blocking the beam of light emitted by the laser, the threshold isn’t met, so the LED light is turned on as an alert signal🚨

Final Project

abstract: Personally I suffers from chronic lower back pain due to a slight lumber disk protrusion😢 so my final project studies the relationship between lower back pressure and the specific angle of sitting posture. This was done by attaching a thrust meter to the subject’ belt and measuring the perpendicular force on their lower back while changing the angle of their sitting posture🪑

results: The experiment shows that the pressure on subjects’ lower back when the angle is 75 degrees is 5 ± 0.2 kg. The pressure when the angle is 60 degrees is 5.5 ± 0.4 kg, which is almost significantly smaller than when the angle is 45 degrees, which is 6.6 ± 1 kg. This is significantly smaller than the perpendicular pressure when the angle is 30 degrees, which is 7.5 ± 1 kg. The difference between each group is statistically significant per an ANOVA and a multiple comparisons test. Therefore, we accept the test hypothesis that the perpendicular pressure on lower back generally increases as the angle between subjects’ back and the bottom of the chair decreases.
